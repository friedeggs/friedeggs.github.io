Occasionally I give talks for some academic reading group or educational event. Here are some of the ones that might be worth browsing:

  • [2021 Apr 7] Generalization of Rules by Neural Nets & Learning Networks of Neurons with Boolean Logic (ML Classics Reading Group)
    Over the pandemic, our computer science PhD cohort organized a fun, informal reading group to take us back to the classics.
    Inspired by all the scaling laws excitement, I coded up a numerical simulation of pre-GPU neural networks for science and leaderboard points!
  • [2020 Sep 18] Differentiable Rendering: Overview (Jiajun Wu's Reading Group)
    A survey talk based on section 2 from this survey paper on Differentiable Rendering.
    Why: 3D vision is a growing subarea within computer vision with some exciting recent advancements, like NeRF
  • [2020 Aug 6] Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models paper review (Uber ATG Reading Group)
    As graciously explained to me by one of the authors, Ajay Jain. (Any errors are mine!)
    Notes

  • [2019 Sep 26] Intro to Statistics for Data Science / Machine Learning (Waterloo Data Science Club)
    The second of two talks on linear algebra and statistics in machine learning, joint with Anzo Teh. Watch here.
  • [2019 July 17] Variational Autoencoders (Waterloo Data Science Club)
    In addition to research and internship opportunities, I learned about AI through reading and summarizing blog posts and papers, then giving talks like this one.
    Watch here.
about this page

As valuable as finished products are, I often find myself wishing I could see the intermediate results that came about in the process of creating a given thing. Sometimes there’s a desire to erase the bridge that led to a solution to “clean things up”, or the practice is to publish that paper in all its glorious academic jargon, or time is limited and getting intermediate results out aren’t worth the effort, but bridges can be helpful and fascinating in their own way whether it’s in revealing an insight or serving as an entertaining biography. Everything’s a potential intermediate result that’s in a state of progress anyway! Sure, you could do some kind of value-added analysis (tweet immediately? crank out an informal blog post? set aside a week to prepare a talk? spend a couple of months developing a paper?) but each of these activities offers something qualitatively different too.

All this is to say, I hope the material on this site can be useful and informative. You’re welcome to contact me about comments, questions, or suggestions. Stay tuned for more.
